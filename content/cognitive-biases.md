| Bias | Category | Definition (with example) |
|---|---|---|
| Action bias | Decision | Tendency to act rather than wait, even when waiting is better. e.g. a goalkeeper diving on every penalty instead of staying in the middle. |
| Actor-observer bias | Causal Attribution | Blaming your own actions on circumstances but others' on their character. e.g. "I was late because of traffic; he was late because he's lazy." |
| Additive bias | Decision | Fixing problems by adding things rather than removing them. e.g. piling more features onto a confusing UI instead of cutting clutter. |
| Aesthetic–usability effect | Estimation | Assuming pretty things work better than they do. e.g. trusting a slick-looking website over a plain but accurate one. |
| Agent detection bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Assuming a hidden person or being caused an event when nothing did. e.g. blaming a ghost when the wind slams a door. |
| Ambiguity effect | Decision | Avoiding options whose probability is unknown. e.g. picking a mediocre known job over a new role with unclear prospects. |
| Anchoring bias | Estimation | Sticking to the first number you hear and barely adjusting. e.g. a high sticker price makes the discounted price feel cheap. |
| Anthropocentric thinking | Opinion Reporting | Interpreting the world through human-centered concepts. e.g. assuming animal behavior must serve human-like goals. |
| Anthropomorphism | Opinion Reporting | Assigning human feelings or intentions to animals or objects. e.g. saying your car "doesn't want to start today." |
| Apophenia | Causal Attribution | Seeing meaningful patterns in random noise. e.g. believing your sneeze predicted the phone call. |
| Assumed similarity bias | Causal Attribution | Assuming others share your traits or opinions. e.g. assuming your team-mate also loves cilantro. |
| Attentional bias | Recall | Attention is pulled toward thoughts that match what's on your mind. e.g. an anxious person noticing every cough on the train. |
| Attribute substitution | Estimation | Answering a hard question by secretly answering an easier one. e.g. judging a stock by how much you like the brand. |
| Authority bias | Decision | Over-weighting opinions of authority figures regardless of content. e.g. trusting a doctor's stock-picking tips. |
| Automation bias | Decision | Over-trusting automated systems even when they're clearly wrong. e.g. driving into a lake because GPS said so. |
| Availability cascade | Hypothesis Assessment | A belief gains credibility just by being repeated publicly. e.g. a viral myth becoming "common knowledge" through endless retweets. |
| Availability heuristic | Estimation | Judging frequency by how easily examples come to mind. e.g. fearing plane crashes because the news keeps showing them. |
| Backfire effect | Opinion Reporting | Correcting someone's false belief sometimes strengthens it. e.g. showing a conspiracy theorist evidence and watching them dig in. |
| Ballot order effect | Decision | Candidates listed first on a ballot tend to get more votes. e.g. an unknown name on top beating better candidates lower down. |
| Bandwagon effect | Opinion Reporting | Believing or doing something because many others already do. e.g. buying a stock just because it's trending. |
| Barnum effect | Hypothesis Assessment | Vague, generic descriptions feel uniquely about you. e.g. "You sometimes doubt yourself" in a horoscope feeling spot-on. |
| Base rate fallacy | Estimation | Ignoring general statistics in favor of specific details. e.g. fearing a rare disease after a positive test, ignoring how rare it is. |
| Belief bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Judging an argument's logic by whether the conclusion sounds true. e.g. accepting a flawed argument because the answer feels right. |
| Ben Franklin effect | Opinion Reporting | You like someone more after doing them a favor. e.g. lending a colleague a book and suddenly feeling friendlier toward them. |
| Berkson's paradox | Hypothesis Assessment | Two unrelated traits look related because of how you sampled. e.g. in hospital, severe diseases and accidents seem linked though they aren't. |
| Bias blind spot | Opinion Reporting | Spotting bias in others but not in yourself. e.g. "Everyone else is tribal; I'm just reasonable." |
| Bizarreness effect | Recall | Weird material is remembered better than ordinary. e.g. recalling an "elephant on a skateboard" joke better than plain ones. |
| Boundary extension | Recall | Recalling a scene as wider than the photo actually showed. e.g. remembering branches that were cropped out of the picture. |
| Cheerleader effect | Decision | People look more attractive in a group than alone. e.g. a friend looking great in the group selfie but average in their solo shot. |
| Childhood amnesia | Recall | Adults can't remember most of their first few years. e.g. having no memory of your second birthday. |
| Choice-supportive bias | Recall | Remembering chosen options as better than they were. e.g. rating your old car as more reliable than it ever actually was. |
| Clustering illusion | Hypothesis Assessment | Seeing streaks or clusters in what is actually random. e.g. expecting tails because a coin just landed heads four times. |
| Cognitive dissonance | Hypothesis Assessment | Adjusting beliefs to escape the discomfort of holding contradictory ones. e.g. a smoker insisting their grandpa smoked and lived to 95. |
| Common source bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Treating multiple reports from the same source as independent confirmation. e.g. three articles all citing one study and calling it "widely reported." |
| Compassion fade | Decision | Caring more about one identifiable victim than many anonymous ones. e.g. donating after one child's photo, ignoring a famine of millions. |
| Compromise effect | Decision | Choosing the middle option just because it's the middle. e.g. ordering medium coffee because small feels stingy and large excessive. |
| Confirmation bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Seeking and remembering evidence that supports what you already believe. e.g. only reading news outlets that agree with your politics. |
| Congruence bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Testing only your favorite hypothesis instead of alternatives. e.g. checking if your code "works" without testing edge cases that could break it. |
| Conjunction fallacy | Estimation | Thinking a specific combination is more likely than one of its parts. e.g. believing "feminist bank teller" is more likely than just "bank teller." |
| Conservatism (Bayesian) bias | Estimation | Updating beliefs too slowly when new evidence arrives. e.g. clinging to "the project's fine" despite three missed deadlines. |
| Conservatism (regressive) bias | Estimation | Remembering extreme values as less extreme than they were. e.g. recalling a record-hot summer as merely "warm." |
| Consistency bias | Recall | Remembering past attitudes as matching your current ones. e.g. swearing you always supported the new policy when you actually opposed it. |
| Context neglect bias | Causal Attribution | Ignoring situational context when judging behavior. e.g. calling a tired surgeon "sloppy" without knowing they'd just done a 20-hour shift. |
| Continued influence effect | Recall | Retracted misinformation keeps shaping your thinking. e.g. still suspecting someone of a crime after they were cleared. |
| Contrast effect | Recall | Things look better or worse depending on what they're next to. e.g. an okay résumé seeming great right after a terrible one. |
| Courtesy bias | Opinion Reporting | Giving polite answers instead of honest ones to avoid offense. e.g. telling your friend their cooking is great when it isn't. |
| Cross-race effect | Recall | Easier to recognize faces of your own race. e.g. struggling to tell apart people from an unfamiliar ethnic group. |
| Cryptomnesia | Recall | Mistaking a remembered idea for an original one. e.g. "inventing" a tune that's actually a song you heard last year. |
| Cue-dependent forgetting | Recall | Forgetting because the right reminder isn't present. e.g. blanking on a fact in the exam that you'd remember instantly at home. |
| Curse of knowledge | Estimation | Forgetting that others don't know what you know. e.g. an expert explaining their field in jargon a beginner can't follow. |
| Declinism | Recall | Believing the past was better and the future will be worse. e.g. "Music was so much better in my day." |
| Decoy effect | Decision | A third bad option makes one of the other two look better. e.g. an overpriced large drink makes the medium feel like great value. |
| Default effect | Decision | Sticking with whatever option is preselected. e.g. far more organ donors in opt-out countries than opt-in. |
| Defensive attribution hypothesis | Causal Attribution | Blaming victims more when their misfortune could happen to you. e.g. "She shouldn't have walked there alone" after a stranger's attack. |
| Denomination effect | Decision | More likely to spend small bills than one large bill of equal total. e.g. blowing five tens but holding onto a single fifty. |
| Disposition effect | Decision | Selling winners too early and holding losers too long. e.g. cashing in a stock up 5% while clinging to one down 40%. |
| Distinction bias | Decision | Differences feel bigger when options are compared side by side. e.g. agonizing over two TVs in store that you couldn't tell apart at home. |
| Domain neglect bias | Causal Attribution | Trusting expertise from one field in unrelated fields. e.g. taking nutrition advice from a software engineer. |
| Doubling-back aversion | Decision | Refusing to retrace a route, even when it's shortest. e.g. driving ten extra minutes instead of turning around. |
| Dread aversion | Decision | Paying extra to skip the anxiety of waiting for a bad event. e.g. taking a stronger shock now to avoid dreading it later. |
| Dunning–Kruger effect | Estimation | People with little skill overestimate their ability. e.g. someone watching a few YouTube videos and feeling ready to wire their house. |
| Duration neglect | Recall | When judging an experience, length matters less than peaks and ends. e.g. rating a long pleasant trip worse than a short one with a great finale. |
| Effort justification | Decision | Valuing things more when you worked hard to get them. e.g. loving the bookshelf you struggled to assemble more than a store-bought one. |
| Egocentric bias | Causal Attribution | Remembering yourself as more central to events than you were. e.g. recalling you "led" a group project where you mostly took notes. |
| Embodiment bias | Causal Attribution | Bodily states shape abstract judgments. e.g. holding a warm drink making a stranger seem friendlier. |
| End-of-history illusion | Opinion Reporting | Believing you've changed a lot but won't change much more. e.g. a 30-year-old certain their tastes are now set for life. |
| Endowment effect | Decision | You value something more once it's yours. e.g. demanding $50 for the mug you wouldn't have paid $10 for. |
| Escalation of commitment | Decision | Throwing more resources at a failing course because of what's already invested. e.g. pouring money into a doomed startup to "save" what's spent. |
| Euphoric recall | Recall | Remembering the highs of an addictive experience and skipping the lows. e.g. an ex-smoker romanticizing cigarettes and forgetting the cough. |
| Exaggerated expectation | Estimation | Real outcomes tend to be less extreme than what we expected. e.g. expecting a movie to be life-changing and finding it merely good. |
| Experimenter's/expectation bias | Causal Attribution | A researcher's hopes subtly nudge the results. e.g. encouraging tones leading subjects to the "right" answer. |
| Extension neglect | Hypothesis Assessment | Ignoring how big or small a group is when judging. e.g. donating the same amount whether 100 or 10,000 birds need rescuing. |
| Extrinsic incentives bias | Estimation | Believing others are more money-driven than you are. e.g. "I work for purpose; my coworkers just want raises." |
| Fading affect bias | Recall | Negative emotions fade faster than positive ones in memory. e.g. recalling a rough trip as mostly fun once a few years pass. |
| False consensus effect | Estimation | Assuming your views are more widely shared than they are. e.g. assuming "everyone" agrees with your political take. |
| False memory | Recall | Remembering something that didn't happen as if it did. e.g. "remembering" being lost in a mall after relatives told the story. |
| False priors | Hypothesis Assessment | Reasoning from incorrect background assumptions. e.g. concluding a friend hates you because you wrongly think they ignored a text. |
| False uniqueness bias | Causal Attribution | Thinking your abilities or virtues are rarer than they are. e.g. assuming your honesty is unusual when most people behave similarly. |
| Fluency heuristic | Hypothesis Assessment | Treating easy-to-process information as more true or important. e.g. trusting a clearly-written rumor over a dense accurate report. |
| Form function attribution bias | Causal Attribution | Assuming things that look similar must work the same way. e.g. expecting a fake plant to filter air like a real one. |
| Framing effect | Decision | The same fact persuades differently depending on wording. e.g. "90% survival rate" feels safer than "10% mortality rate." |
| Frequency illusion | Recall | After you notice something once, you suddenly see it everywhere. e.g. buying a red Honda and now spotting them on every street. |
| Functional fixedness | Decision | Seeing objects only as their typical use. e.g. not realizing a coin can work as a screwdriver. |
| Fundamental attribution error | Causal Attribution | Blaming others' behavior on character, your own on circumstances. e.g. "He cut me off because he's a jerk; I cut in because I was rushing." |
| G. I. Joe fallacy | Causal Attribution | Believing that knowing about a bias is enough to overcome it. e.g. learning about anchoring and still being anchored by the next price you see. |
| Gambler's fallacy | Estimation | Thinking past random outcomes change future ones. e.g. expecting tails because the coin just landed heads five times. |
| Gender bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Judging or treating people unfairly based on gender. e.g. assuming a tech leader is a man when reading their email. |
| Gender differences in eyewitness memory | Recall | Men and women tend to recall different aspects of the same event. e.g. one witness remembers what the robber wore, another what was stolen. |
| Generation effect | Recall | You remember things better if you generated them yourself. e.g. recalling vocabulary you tested yourself on, not just re-read. |
| Google effect | Recall | Forgetting things you know you can look up. e.g. not bothering to remember phone numbers because they're in your contacts. |
| Group attribution error | Causal Attribution | Treating one person's traits as representative of their whole group. e.g. assuming a rude tourist means "all those people are rude." |
| Groupshift | Hypothesis Assessment | Groups make more extreme decisions than individuals would alone. e.g. a cautious team agreeing on a wildly risky bet in the meeting. |
| Groupthink | Hypothesis Assessment | Group harmony overrides honest debate, leading to bad decisions. e.g. a team launching a doomed product because no one wanted to dissent. |
| Halo effect | Opinion Reporting | One positive trait colors your judgment of unrelated ones. e.g. assuming the good-looking candidate is also smarter. |
| Hard–easy effect | Estimation | Overconfident on hard tasks, underconfident on easy ones. e.g. sure you'll ace a tough exam, doubting yourself on a quiz you'd ace blindfolded. |
| Hedonic recall bias | Estimation | Misremembering past pleasure or pain levels. e.g. forgetting how miserable last summer's heat was until it returns. |
| Hindsight bias | Recall | After learning the outcome, you feel you knew it all along. e.g. "I always said that startup would fail" — said only after it did. |
| Hostile attribution bias | Causal Attribution | Reading hostile intent into ambiguous behavior. e.g. assuming someone bumped you on purpose when it was an accident. |
| Hot-cold empathy gap | Estimation | Underestimating how you'll feel or act in a different emotional/physical state. e.g. shopping while hungry and buying way too much. |
| Hot-hand fallacy | Estimation | Believing success streaks predict more success. e.g. betting heavily on a basketball player who just made three shots. |
| Humor effect | Recall | Funny material is remembered better than non-funny. e.g. recalling the joke from a presentation but forgetting the stats. |
| Hyperbolic discounting | Decision | Strongly preferring smaller rewards now over bigger ones later. e.g. taking $50 today rather than $100 next month. |
| Illusion of asymmetric insight | Opinion Reporting | Thinking you know others better than they know you. e.g. "I can read my coworkers like a book; they have no clue who I really am." |
| Illusion of control | Causal Attribution | Believing you influence outcomes that are actually random. e.g. blowing on dice for "luck." |
| Illusion of explanatory depth | Hypothesis Assessment | Thinking you understand something until asked to explain it. e.g. confidently knowing how a zipper works — until you try to describe it. |
| Illusion of learning | Opinion Reporting | Feeling you've learned something just from re-reading or watching. e.g. nodding through a lecture and bombing the quiz next day. |
| Illusion of transparency | Estimation | Overestimating how visible your inner state is to others. e.g. sure everyone can see you're nervous when no one notices. |
| Illusion of validity | Estimation | Confidence in predictions the data doesn't actually support. e.g. an interviewer sure they can spot the best hire in 10 minutes. |
| Illusory correlation | Hypothesis Assessment / Causal Attribution | Seeing a link between two things that aren't actually related. e.g. believing rain follows car washes. |
| Illusory superiority | Opinion Reporting | Thinking you're above average at most things. e.g. 80% of drivers rating themselves "better than average." |
| Illusory truth effect | Hypothesis Assessment | Repeated statements feel truer just from being repeated. e.g. an oft-heard slogan starting to seem factual. |
| Impact bias | Estimation | Overestimating how strong and long-lasting future feelings will be. e.g. believing a promotion will make you happy "forever." |
| Implicit association | Recall | Automatic mental links between concepts, often outside awareness. e.g. unconsciously pairing "doctor" with "man." |
| Impostor Syndrome | Opinion Reporting | Feeling like a fraud despite real competence. e.g. a senior engineer convinced colleagues will "find out" she doesn't belong. |
| Information bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Seeking more information even when it can't change the decision. e.g. ordering a fifth test that can't alter the treatment. |
| Ingroup bias | Causal Attribution | Favoring people from your own group. e.g. rating your team's idea as better just because it's your team's. |
| Insensitivity to sample size | Estimation | Treating small and large samples as equally reliable. e.g. trusting a survey of 5 people as much as one of 5,000. |
| Intentionality bias | Causal Attribution | Defaulting to "they did it on purpose" for ambiguous actions. e.g. assuming the driver who splashed you was deliberate. |
| Interoceptive bias | Estimation | Misreading internal body signals when estimating things. e.g. blaming anxiety on the room being "stuffy." |
| Just-world fallacy | Causal Attribution | Believing people get what they deserve. e.g. "He must have done something to end up homeless." |
| Lag effect | Recall | Memory is better when study sessions are spread out over time. e.g. learning vocabulary over 10 days, not crammed into 1. |
| Law of the instrument | Decision | Over-relying on the tool you know best. e.g. "If all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail." |
| Less-is-better effect | Decision | A smaller item can seem more valuable than a bigger one when judged alone. e.g. a small generous-looking ice cream beating a half-empty big one. |
| Levels-of-processing effect | Recall | Deeper, meaning-focused study is remembered better than shallow. e.g. recalling words you used in sentences over ones you just spelled. |
| Leveling and sharpening | Recall | In retelling, some details fade and others get exaggerated. e.g. a fish story growing larger every time it's told. |
| List-length effect | Recall | The longer the list, the smaller the share you recall. e.g. remembering 7/10 items but only 12/30. |
| Loss aversion | Decision | Losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. e.g. losing $50 stinging more than finding $50 thrills. |
| Memory inhibition | Recall | Retrieving one memory can suppress related ones. e.g. naming one old teacher blanks you on the others. |
| Mere exposure effect | Decision | You prefer things just because you've seen them before. e.g. liking a song more after the third play. |
| Misinformation effect | Recall | Post-event information distorts the original memory. e.g. a leading question making a witness recall a stop sign that wasn't there. |
| Misinterpreted-effort hypothesis | Opinion Reporting | Confusing how hard someone tried with how good their work is. e.g. praising a teammate's "great work" because they stayed late. |
| Modality effect | Recall | Spoken final items are remembered better than written ones. e.g. catching the last item on a verbal grocery list better than a written one. |
| Money illusion | Decision | Thinking in face-value money, ignoring inflation. e.g. feeling rich about a raise that doesn't keep up with price rises. |
| Mood-congruent memory bias | Recall | You recall things that match your current mood. e.g. on a sad day, only past failures come to mind. |
| Moral credential effect | Opinion Reporting | Past good acts make people feel licensed to do questionable ones. e.g. "I gave to charity, so I can be rude to the waiter." |
| Moral luck | Opinion Reporting | Judging morality by outcome rather than action. e.g. condemning the drunk driver who hit someone, while another lucky drunk driver gets home and is seen as fine. |
| Motonormativity | Causal Attribution | Treating car-centric assumptions as the default and not noticing them. e.g. "Why don't they just drive?" — unaware not everyone can. |
| Naïve cynicism | Estimation | Expecting more selfishness in others than there actually is. e.g. assuming the volunteer must have an ulterior motive. |
| Naïve realism | Opinion Reporting | Believing you see reality objectively and others are biased. e.g. "I just see things as they are; the other side has an agenda." |
| Negativity bias | Recall / Opinion Reporting | Bad events stick in mind more than good ones. e.g. one harsh review overshadowing nine positive ones. |
| Neglect of probability | Decision | Ignoring odds and reacting to the worst-case scenario. e.g. fearing shark attacks at the beach despite the near-zero risk. |
| Next-in-line effect | Recall | You forget what someone said just before your turn to speak. e.g. blanking on the name introduced right before you spoke yours. |
| Non-adaptive choice switching | Decision | Switching options after a bad outcome even when the original was right. e.g. abandoning a good strategy after one unlucky loss. |
| Normalcy bias | Decision | Underestimating disasters because they haven't happened to you. e.g. ignoring evacuation orders because "this neighborhood's always been fine." |
| Not invented here | Decision | Rejecting outside ideas in favor of your own. e.g. a team rebuilding a library that already exists because it isn't theirs. |
| Objectivity illusion | Causal Attribution | Believing your perceptions are objective truths. e.g. "I'm not biased — I'm just realistic." |
| Observer-expectancy effect | Hypothesis Assessment | A researcher's expectations subtly skew results. e.g. encouraging cues only given to subjects in the "treatment" group. |
| Omission bias | Opinion Reporting | Harm from inaction feels less wrong than equal harm from action. e.g. preferring to let a sick patient die naturally over an intervention with the same risk. |
| Optimism bias | Estimation | Expecting good things to happen to you more than to others. e.g. assuming you won't get divorced even though half do. |
| Ostrich effect | Causal Attribution | Avoiding negative information by ignoring it. e.g. refusing to open the credit card bill. |
| Outcome bias | Estimation | Judging a decision by its result, not by the decision itself. e.g. praising a reckless gamble that happened to pay off. |
| Outgroup favoritism | Causal Attribution | Favoring people outside your own group. e.g. members of a low-status group preferring outsiders. |
| Outgroup homogeneity bias | Estimation | "They're all the same; we're individuals." e.g. lumping all fans of a rival team into one stereotype. |
| Overconfidence effect | Hypothesis Assessment | Subjective confidence higher than actual accuracy. e.g. being 90% sure of trivia answers you only get right 60% of the time. |
| Pareidolia | Hypothesis Assessment | Seeing meaningful patterns (especially faces) in random shapes. e.g. spotting a face in a power outlet. |
| Part-list cueing effect | Recall | Being shown some items of a list makes the rest harder to recall. e.g. seeing half your grocery list and forgetting the other half. |
| Peak–end rule | Recall | We judge experiences by their peak and how they ended. e.g. rating a vacation by the highlight and the last day, ignoring boring middle days. |
| Perky effect | Recall | A real faint image can be mistaken for imagination. e.g. dismissing a faintly projected picture as your own mental image. |
| Persistence | Recall | Unwanted memories keep popping into your mind. e.g. flashbacks of an embarrassing moment that won't quit. |
| Pessimism bias | Estimation | Overestimating chances of bad outcomes. e.g. a depressed person convinced everything will go wrong. |
| Phantom effect | Decision | An unavailable option still influences your choice. e.g. a sold-out item making the remaining one look better. |
| Picture superiority effect | Recall | Pictures are remembered better than words. e.g. recalling the chart long after forgetting the caption. |
| Placement bias | Recall | Past performance is remembered as better than it actually was. e.g. recalling your last quarter as stronger than the numbers say. |
| Plan continuation bias | Decision | Sticking to a plan despite changing conditions. e.g. a pilot landing in worsening weather instead of diverting. |
| Planning fallacy | Estimation | Underestimating how long tasks will take. e.g. swearing the essay will take two hours; it takes eight. |
| Plant blindness | Causal Attribution | Not noticing plants in your environment. e.g. describing a forest photo without mentioning trees. |
| Positivity effect | Recall | Older adults remember positives more than negatives. e.g. grandparents recalling a tough childhood as mostly sweet. |
| Present bias | Decision | Strongly favoring the near term over the future. e.g. skipping the gym today to enjoy now, regretting it for years. |
| Prevention bias | Decision | Overvaluing prevention efforts compared with equally good treatments. e.g. funding prevention campaigns while underfunding hospitals. |
| Primacy effect | Recall | First items in a list are remembered best. e.g. recalling the first speakers at a meeting but not the middle ones. |
| Pro-innovation bias | Causal Attribution | Overvaluing new things and ignoring their downsides. e.g. assuming the new app is better just because it's new. |
| Probability matching | Hypothesis Assessment | Matching response frequencies to outcome probabilities, even when one option is always better. e.g. picking the rarer side 30% of the time on a 70/30 task instead of always the 70% one. |
| Processing difficulty effect | Recall | Information that's harder to read is sometimes remembered better. e.g. recalling lecture notes more easily in an ugly font. |
| Projection bias | Decision | Assuming your future self will want what you want now. e.g. grocery shopping when hungry and buying enough for a week. |
| Proportionality bias | Causal Attribution | Big events need big causes — even when they don't. e.g. believing a president's death must involve a conspiracy. |
| Pseudocertainty effect | Decision | Treating likely outcomes as certain, especially in multi-stage choices. e.g. valuing a "guaranteed" stage 2 result when stage 1 isn't guaranteed at all. |
| Puritanical bias | Causal Attribution | Blaming bad outcomes on moral failings. e.g. assuming poverty must mean laziness. |
| Pygmalion effect | Causal Attribution | High expectations from others improve performance. e.g. students perform better when teachers expect them to. |
| Quantification bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Trusting numbers over harder-to-measure information. e.g. judging employees only by metrics, ignoring teamwork. |
| Reactance | Decision | Doing the opposite when you feel pressured. e.g. refusing to wear the jacket the moment your mum tells you to. |
| Reactive devaluation | Decision | Devaluing a proposal just because of who proposed it. e.g. rejecting an idea solely because a rival suggested it. |
| Recency effect | Recall | The last items in a list are remembered best. e.g. recalling the last few groceries but not the middle. |
| Recency illusion | Recall | Thinking something is new just because you only just noticed it. e.g. "Kids today suddenly say 'literally' wrong" — they've said it that way for decades. |
| Reminiscence bump | Recall | Adults remember their adolescent and early-adult years most vividly. e.g. a 70-year-old recalling more from their 20s than their 50s. |
| Repetition blindness | Recall | When the same word repeats quickly, you may miss the second one. e.g. reading "the the cat" and only seeing one "the." |
| Restraint bias | Estimation | Overestimating your ability to resist temptation. e.g. keeping cookies in the house, sure you won't eat them — then eating them. |
| Rhyme as reason effect | Hypothesis Assessment | Rhyming statements feel more believable. e.g. "If it doesn't fit, you must acquit" landing harder than the unrhymed version. |
| Risk compensation | Decision | Safer feeling = riskier behavior. e.g. drivers in cars with anti-lock brakes drive faster. |
| Rosy retrospection | Recall | Remembering the past as better than it was. e.g. fondly recalling the camping trip you complained through. |
| Salience bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Focusing on what stands out and ignoring quieter signals. e.g. one dramatic news story shaping your view of a whole industry. |
| Saying is believing effect | Hypothesis Assessment | Tailoring a message to listeners makes you believe it yourself. e.g. softening a story for a friend, then remembering it that softer way. |
| Scope neglect | Decision | Feeling-driven judgments ignore the scale of the problem. e.g. donating the same amount whether 10 or 10,000 birds need saving. |
| Selection bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Sampling in a way that skews conclusions. e.g. surveying only Twitter users about what "people" think. |
| Selective perception | Causal Attribution | Noticing only what fits your expectations. e.g. fans of opposing teams seeing the same call totally differently. |
| Self-relevance effect | Recall | You remember information better when it relates to you. e.g. recalling words you sorted "describes me / doesn't" better than other word lists. |
| Self-serving bias | Causal Attribution | Taking credit for wins, blaming losses on outside factors. e.g. "I aced the test thanks to me; I bombed it because of bad questions." |
| Semmelweis reflex | Decision | Rejecting new evidence that contradicts established norms. e.g. doctors initially mocking the idea of washing hands before surgery. |
| Serial position effect | Recall | First and last items are remembered better than middle ones. e.g. remembering the opening and ending of a speech but not the middle. |
| Sexual overperception/underperception bias | Estimation | Misreading how sexually interested the other person is. e.g. a man reading politeness as flirtation, or a woman missing genuine interest. |
| Shared information bias | Decision | Groups discuss what everyone already knows and skip unique info. e.g. a meeting recapping common knowledge while ignoring one person's key insight. |
| Social comparison bias | Decision | Disfavoring people whose strengths threaten yours. e.g. a hiring manager avoiding candidates who'd outshine them. |
| Social cryptomnesia | Recall | Forgetting that a now-accepted social change was once controversial. e.g. assuming everyone "always" supported a movement that was once unpopular. |
| Social desirability bias | Opinion Reporting | Answering to look good rather than honestly. e.g. underreporting alcohol use to a researcher. |
| Source confusion | Recall | Remembering content but not where it came from. e.g. recalling a fact but not whether it was a friend or a tabloid. |
| Spacing effect | Recall | Spaced study beats cramming. e.g. 30 min/day for a week beats 4 hours the night before. |
| Spotlight effect | Estimation | Overestimating how much others notice you. e.g. obsessing over your shirt stain that nobody else saw. |
| Status quo bias | Decision | Preferring things to stay as they are. e.g. sticking with the same overpriced phone plan year after year. |
| Stereotype bias | Recall | Memory shifts toward stereotype-consistent details. e.g. recalling a doctor as a man even when the doctor was a woman. |
| Stereotyping | Opinion Reporting | Assuming individuals match the traits of their group. e.g. expecting an accountant to be dull. |
| Subadditivity effect | Estimation / Hypothesis Assessment | Probabilities of parts add up to more than the whole. e.g. listing causes of death separately inflates the estimated total risk. |
| Subjective validation | Hypothesis Assessment | Believing vague claims because they feel personally meaningful. e.g. accepting a fortune-teller because one statement felt eerily accurate. |
| Suffix effect | Recall | A meaningless tag-on after a list weakens recall of the last item. e.g. forgetting the last grocery item because someone said "thanks" right after. |
| Suggestibility | Recall | Outside suggestions can reshape your memory. e.g. accepting a friend's version of events and "remembering" it that way. |
| Surrogation | Causal Attribution | Confusing a metric with the thing it measures. e.g. managers chasing test scores and forgetting they were proxies for learning. |
| Survivorship bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Looking only at successes and ignoring the failures. e.g. studying successful startups and missing what killed the many that died. |
| System justification | Causal Attribution | Defending the current system even when it harms you. e.g. supporting an unfair status quo you personally lose under. |
| Systematic bias | Estimation | Consistent errors that lean one way. e.g. a scale that always reads 2 kg too high. |
| Tachypsychia | Estimation | Time seems to slow or speed up in high-emotion moments. e.g. a car crash feeling like it took ages but lasting one second. |
| Teleological bias | Causal Attribution | Assuming things exist for a purpose. e.g. believing rivers exist "to give us water." |
| Telescoping effect | Recall | Misjudging when an event happened — recent feels far, far feels recent. e.g. swearing a memory was two years ago when it was five. |
| Testing effect | Recall | Self-testing improves retention more than re-reading. e.g. recalling flashcards better than the textbook you reread. |
| Third-person effect | Opinion Reporting | Believing media affects others more than yourself. e.g. "Ads don't work on me — but they sure work on everyone else." |
| Time-saving bias | Estimation | Misjudging how much time is saved by speeding up. e.g. speeding from 60 to 80 mph thinking it saves more time than it does. |
| Tip of the tongue phenomenon | Recall | Feeling sure you know a word but unable to retrieve it. e.g. having the actor's name just out of reach. |
| Trait ascription bias | Opinion Reporting | Seeing others as fixed-trait, yourself as flexible. e.g. "He's stubborn; I just adapt to context." |
| Travis syndrome | Estimation | Overestimating your era's importance compared with other periods. e.g. believing today's tech is the biggest leap in history. |
| Truth bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Defaulting to believing others are telling the truth. e.g. taking a stranger's story at face value. |
| Turkey illusion | Causal Attribution | Treating a long run of good times as proof it'll continue. e.g. a turkey concluding humans love it — right up to Thanksgiving. |
| Ultimate attribution error | Causal Attribution | Group-level fundamental attribution error. e.g. "Their successes are luck; our successes are skill." |
| Unconscious bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Prejudgments you hold without knowing it. e.g. unconsciously favoring candidates whose names sound familiar. |
| Unit bias | Estimation | Treating one serving/portion as the "right" amount, regardless of size. e.g. finishing the whole bagel even though it's twice normal size. |
| Value selection bias | Hypothesis Assessment | Cherry-picking values or metrics that support a position. e.g. quoting only the survey that backs your point. |
| Verbatim effect | Recall | Remembering the gist of something rather than exact words. e.g. recalling a story's plot but not a single sentence. |
| von Restorff effect | Recall | Distinctive items in a list stand out and are remembered. e.g. the one red word in a list of black ones being unforgettable. |
| Weber–Fechner law | Estimation | Perceived changes shrink as the original quantity grows. e.g. adding $1 to a $5 price feels big; adding $1 to $500 feels nothing. |
| Well travelled road effect | Decision | Familiar routes feel shorter than unfamiliar ones. e.g. the drive home feeling quicker than the drive there. |
| Women are wonderful effect | Opinion Reporting | Women are evaluated more positively than men overall. e.g. ascribing more kindness and warmth to women on average. |
| Worse-than-average effect | Estimation | On hard tasks, people think they're below average. e.g. most people rating themselves as worse-than-average jugglers. |
| Zeigarnik effect | Recall | Interrupted or unfinished tasks stick in memory better. e.g. a half-written email nagging you all evening. |
| Zero-risk bias | Decision | Preferring to eliminate one small risk entirely over big reductions in larger ones. e.g. spending heavily to remove a 1% risk instead of cutting a 50% risk to 30%. |
| Zero-sum bias | Opinion Reporting | Assuming one person's gain must be another's loss. e.g. believing immigrants must take jobs from locals when the economy can grow. |
