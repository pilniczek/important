In electronics, the [signal-to-noise ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio) compares the strength of a meaningful signal against the background noise on the same channel. The term is *"sometimes used metaphorically to refer to the ratio of useful information to false or irrelevant data in a conversation or exchange"* — and once you start looking, the same mechanism shows up anywhere.

## The mechanism

### Signal

The information or situation that actually matters in context.

### Noise

Anything competing for attention. Cheap to create. It is either **Natural** or **Intentional**. The expensive mistake is mixing them up: treat intentional as natural (don't defend enough) or treat natural as intentional (paranoia, burn trust). Many [[cognitive-biases]] (availability heuristic, attentional bias, confirmation bias) make it worse by making noise *feel* like signal.

#### Natural noise

Random variation, background chatter, situations that didn't take you into account, a busy world.

#### Intentional noise

Created on purpose — advertisers, manipulators, misinformation. Adaptable to your filters. Stepping back, doubting what you're shown, and cutting the source's hold on you handle it.

### Threshold

Below a certain ratio the signal is lost regardless of absolute volume. Valuable information surrounded by a hundred fake ones just becomes noise. The ratio gets worse on its own unless someone actively maintains it.

## Levers

Four moves raise the ratio, roughly from smallest to biggest impact:

1. **Boost signal** — be specific, repeat the things that actually matter, lead with the headline.
2. **Cut noise** — mute, unsubscribe, delete, say no. The cheapest fix is usually taking things away, not adding more.
3. **Filter at the source** — a short list of trusted sources beats a huge feed even with a clever filter on top.
4. **Raise your own threshold** — don't react to every fluctuation. Often the best move is to wait until the signal repeats before responding.
